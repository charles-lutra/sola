The fundamental challenge

Getting inventory right from day one

The core tension: over-ordering ties up scarce capital in stock sitting in a 3PL warehouse. Under-ordering means stockouts, disappointed customers, and missed BNPL revenue — with a 25–35 day sea freight lead time meaning you can't fix it quickly. For Sola, the bias should be slightly toward holding more stock than you think you need in year one.

25–35 days
China → Felixstowe. The number everything else is built around. You must see a stockout coming a month before it happens.

50× Sola 400 Hero kits + 20× Sola 800 Pro kits. Enough to run a proper launch without overcommitting capital.
Why under-ordering is worse
48hrs
Klarna pays Sola within 48hrs of each order. You can only collect that revenue if stock exists. A stockout = lost sale, lost customer, lost BNPL income.
The asymmetry matters: A viral TikTok or MoneySavingExpert mention could generate 30 orders in 48 hours. Air freight to top up is available but costly — roughly 8–10× sea freight per unit. Your stockout protection is a good reorder trigger, not just more stock.
Inventory fundamentals

The three concepts you need to know

Reorder Point (ROP) Formula
ROP = (Average Daily Sales × Lead Time in Days) + Safety Stock
Example — Sola 400 Hero at 2 units/day
ROP = (2 × 30 days) + 14 days buffer = 74 units
1. Reorder Point
The stock level at which you trigger a new purchase order. Hit this number and you order immediately — not when you run out. With 30-day sea freight, you need to act a month early.
2. Safety Stock
A buffer held above your expected sales during lead time to absorb variability — a delayed shipment or a sudden demand spike. For Sola year one, target 2–3 weeks of expected sales as your buffer.
3. Order Quantity
How much to order when you reorder. In early stage: how much can I sell before the next shipment arrives, minus safety stock? That figure becomes your order size. It grows as your velocity data improves.
The honest reality for a brand-new business: All of this requires sales data you don't have yet. Your first 1–2 orders are educated guesses, and that's completely normal. The goal in year one is to learn your demand pattern, not to optimise perfectly. Make small enough orders that a mistake doesn't sink you.

Reorder decision

Simple framework for every reorder decision

Until you have 3–6 months of real sales data, use this four-question process each time you consider placing a new order. The inventory spreadsheet (to be built in Google Sheets) will automate this — you enter current stock and recent sales, it tells you whether to order and how much.

1

What have I sold in the last 30 days, by SKU?

This is your observed daily run rate. Divide by 30 to get average daily sales. Track this from day one — even a simple tally in a spreadsheet. This data is the foundation of every future order decision.

2

How many days until a new shipment would arrive if I ordered today?

Default assumption: 30 days sea freight + 3–5 days UK clearance and 3PL receiving = ~35 days total. Add buffer for Chinese New Year, port delays, and supplier lead time. Round up to 40 days to be safe.

3

Do I have enough stock to cover that period, plus a 2–3 week buffer?

Multiply your daily run rate by 40 (lead time) + 14–21 (buffer) = your reorder threshold. If current stock is above this, wait. If at or below, order now.

4

If ordering — how much?

Order enough to cover your next 60–90 days of expected sales, minus what you'll still have when the new shipment arrives. Factor in any upcoming seasonal peaks or marketing campaigns that might spike demand.

The honest answer on year one: You will make sub-optimal decisions. The goal isn't perfection — it's making mistakes cheaply. Keep orders small enough that an error doesn't sink you, but large enough that you don't constantly run out. Your 50-unit first order sits squarely in that zone.
Infrastructure

Systems, 3PL, and what connects to what

Shopify (built-in inventory)
Sufficient for launch. Tracks stock per SKU, decrements on each sale, blocks overselling. Set low-stock email alerts. Enable "stop selling when out of stock." Free — already included in your Shopify subscription.
3PL ↔ Shopify integration
Your most critical integration. Orders auto-push from Shopify to 3PL on placement. 3PL updates Shopify with tracking numbers on dispatch. Stock levels sync both ways. Confirm this integration works before signing with any 3PL.
Inventory Planner (later)
~£99–199/mo. Shopify-native demand forecasting and reorder point calculation. Worth adding when you're moving 80–100+ units/month. Not needed at launch — Shopify built-in + a spreadsheet covers it.

Longer-term strategy

How inventory strategy evolves as Sola scales

Most DTC hardware brands follow a predictable progression. Companies like EcoFlow, Anker, and BioLite stayed on 3PL or Amazon FBA for years before bringing anything in-house. The ones that warehoused too early consistently cite it as a capital mistake.

Launch → ~£500k revenue · Stage 1
3PL — no debate
Outsource everything. Pay per pick/pack/store. No fixed costs, no lease, no staff. Negotiate good rates upfront — volume commitments in exchange for lower per-unit fees. The 3PL relationship is a genuine asset if you pick the right partner. Use Shopify built-in inventory + a Google Sheets reorder tracker.
~£500k–1.5m revenue · Stage 2
Larger 3PL + Amazon FBA as parallel channel
At this stage you have a real decision. Options: (a) negotiate a semi-dedicated 3PL bay giving you faster turnaround and more control without fixed cost; (b) add Amazon FBA as a second fulfilment channel — opens a huge new customer base, their network handles bulky goods well, though the 15% referral fee + FBA costs need to model out; (c) add a small UK lock-up (£300–600/mo) as a secondary reserve for returns, influencer seeding, and trade show stock — not primary fulfilment.
£1.5m+ revenue, 300+ units/month · Stage 3
Dedicated 3PL contract or own warehouse — revisit properly
Only now does the own-warehouse conversation become real. Even then, many brands choose a dedicated 3PL contract (where the 3PL operates a semi-exclusive facility for you) rather than full ownership — you get control without capital and management overhead. A leased industrial unit in Greater London costs £15,000–40,000/year before rates, insurance, utilities, and staff. You'd need 80–100+ units/month just to break even on the warehouse versus continuing with 3PL.
Recommended path for Sola: 3PL for the first 18–24 months → Amazon FBA as an additional channel around month 12 if sales justify it → revisit own warehousing at £1m+ revenue. The founders who warehouse too early almost always regret it. Stay asset-light; spend the capital on growth instead.
